The Monty Hall Problem

Look at these three doors...

You are on a game show. Behind one of these doors is a new car. Behind two others are piles of coal. When the host asks you to pick one of the doors, you choose the left door at random. Then, the host opens the right door with coal behind it, and asks if you want to switch your choice. If you want a new car, would it be advantageous to switch?

Probabilities

Presumed Probabilities of Each Door Containing a Car
Left Door Middle Door Right Door
50.0% 50.0% 0.0%

One might think not, considering you are choosing between two doors. Surely, this situation means there is a 50% chance of getting a car regardless of what door you finally choose. However, the real answer is much more unintuitive. It turns out that there is an advantage to switching from your initial choice to the new door! Let's explore the reason as to why. When you chose a door at random in the beginning, you had a one-third or approximately 33.3% chance of getting the car. That probability does not change when a new door is revealed! It remains 33.3% and does not increase to 50%. The key to understanding this puzzle is that nothing changes behind the door you chose just because another door has opened. Now that the right door is open, the question essentially becomes, "Was it more likely the car was behind the door I chose, or any of the other doors?" Clearly the latter is more likely, and therefore the chance of getting a new car by switching is 66.6%!

True Probabilities of Each Door Containing a Car
Left Door Middle Door Door Door
33.3% 66.6% 0.0%
